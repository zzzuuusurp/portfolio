3. Challenges Faced / Solutions

  • The Problem: The chart refused to update after creating or updating a new expense.
  • The Solution: After being reviewed by my peers and Fast, I found out that I forgot to add event listeners for my chart, which led to ineffectiveness. After adding the event listener, it resulted in a functional product.
  • The Problem: Searching for a career only showed the career if the term was exact
  • The Solution: After searching for the specifics of filter() and finding other examples of a search bar online, I discovered that I was meant to use contains() instead. After fixing that, the bar worked as expected.
  • The Problem: The “balance remaining” was always red, even when the amount was in the positives
  • The Solution: rather than being a JavaScript issue like I thought, i found out through console.logs and manual review that the issue was that the classes I was assigning had the same red color instead of red and green. Once that was fixed, my balance accurately showed if you had money remaining at a glance.
  • The Problem: adding a random color for a new expense instead randomized a color for every expense, making it so that the chart dramatically changed color every time something was updated.
  • The Solution: I pinpointed that the issue was how I used the random color function instead of a list of colors. Once I made the random color push into a list every time a new expense was added, the colors remained the same as expected.
  • The Problem: The product, when trying to line them up, sucked at being lined up
  • The Solution: I switched from using table to CSS grid, as it allowed for better flexibility when making small adjustments
  • The Problem: I had no prior knowledge of Javascript before adding the cart feature, which led to the system I used being very incompatible with my page.
  • The Solution: I consulted things that did know JavaScript, such as Mr. Fast and Gemini, when something with javascript went wrong. This allowed me to understand more about what went wrong with my features and how to fix them.
  • The Problem: I was not proud of my color pallette
  • The Solution: I looked online for colors that fit an aesthetic or paired well with a certain color. This led to a more visually appealing website in terms of colors.
  • The Problem: Drag and drop didn't seem to fix prioritizing
  • The Solution: I rewrote my list code from scratch, separating prioritized and non-prioritized items into two arrays and then combining them into one big one. This ended up fixing the problem, and let the prioritized items stay up top.
  • The Problem: I didn' fully understand how to filter and create DOMs
  • The Solution: I had to look up on resources like W3Schools how DOMs worked, and I ended up getting better with practice, which led me to the end result of a functional list that updates properly.
  • The Problem: There was a lot of whitespace when there were no items.
  • The Solution: I thought about ways that would fit the theme, and ended up adding a delete feature, where you delete items by dropping them in the garbage truck. This helped give the list some personality and also make the list more intuitive.

4. Habits of Mind in Action

  • Investigate: I spent the first week of the project investigating what other budget calculator websites did. I found some similarities, good features, and flaws, which allowed me to design something that I felt met the client's needs.
  • Communication: The first two weeks consisted of me telling a developer on what to do rather than coding by myself. I effectively communicated well enough for my project to meet most of the criteria, which allowed me to make personal adjustments by the time I was able to code myself.
  • Persist: Chart.js was a pain to effectively integrate into my product. It was the thing that I had to spend the most time debugging and testing out of the entire project in order for it to work as well as it does.
  • Investigate: I looked online at how other “competitors” to our repellant company had their websites, and I mirrored the parts I thought were good and changed the parts I thought wouldn't work as well.
  • Persist: I spent around a week fine-tuning the CSS layouts and colors, alongside another week getting the Javascript to work with my cart and products.
  • Reflection: I came back to my project a couple of months later and improved it by fixing layout issues and adding the cart feature, which was not originally there.
  • Reflect: This list had me think a lot about how I wanted to present the list itself to others. The end result was a construction theme, with a garbage dump being the key to deletion.
  • Persist: Getting the drag and drop to work was a massive pain that took the majority of my time to get working. It took around 4 days for everything to work the way I wanted it to.
